BIO

Welcome! I am an Assistant Professor in the Department of Political Science at Emory University. Previously, I was a Postdoctoral Prize Research Fellow in Politics at Nuffield College, University of Oxford, and an Associate at University of Oxford China Centre.

My research sits at the intersection of comparative politics and international relations, with a focus on how political communication and information manipulation by authoritarian regimes shapes what citizens believe and how they behave. Most of my work centers on China, with several projects extending to Vietnam, Malaysia, Hungary, Poland, Türkiye, and Taiwan. Specifically, my research investigates how authoritarian regimes operate censorship and propaganda to manipulate domestic and international information environments, while generating public support for these apparatuses. A second strand examines how foreign policy events and international conflicts shape public opinion on the use of military force. Recently, I have also been investigating how authoritarian and autocratizing regimes communicate democratic backsliding, and its downstream consequences for democratic values. Beyond this core agenda on authoritarian politics and political communication, I am also interested in prejudice toward LGBTQ individuals and its political consequences. Methodologically, I adopt a multi-method approach, including computational methods (text-as-data and audio-as-data), survey experiments, design-based causal inference, and qualitative fieldwork. My work has been published in the Journal of Politics, International Organization, Comparative Political Studies, Political Science Research and Methods, and Public Opinion Quarterly. My Dissertation, "Essays on Censorship and Public Opinion in Authoritarian Regimes," won the 2025 APSA Thomas E. Patterson Best Dissertation Award in Political Communication.

In 2024, I earned my Ph.D. in Political Science at Washington University in St. Louis. In 2018, I received an LL.B. degree in Law from Renmin University of China.
